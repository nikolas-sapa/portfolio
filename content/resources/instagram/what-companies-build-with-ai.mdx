Mythos got released. Then it got banned. The hype was everywhere for a week.

And here's the thing nobody says out loud: most companies still have no idea what to do with any of it. Not the solopreneurs. Not the industry leaders either. They have access to the most capable models in history and they're using them to summarize emails.

The gap isn't the model. The gap is what you do with it.

I spent the last while studying the top 100 companies actually getting value out of AI today — not the ones posting about it, the ones shipping with it. Three things separate them from everyone else. None of them are about which model you pick.

## 1. They stopped watching and started building

The default move when a new model drops is to consume. Watch the breakdown video. Read the thread. Bookmark the prompt pack. Watch four more videos.

The companies getting real value skipped that. Consumption is not capability. You don't learn what AI can do for *your* work by watching someone else use it for theirs. You learn it by putting it on a real task and watching where it breaks.

Every hour you spend watching a tutorial is an hour you didn't spend finding out what the thing actually does in your hands. The best teams treat models like a new hire on day one: give it real work, see what comes back, correct it, repeat.

## 2. They work *with* the AI — they build teams and let them explore

This is the real unlock, and it's where almost everyone stops short.

The losers use AI like a vending machine. One prompt, one answer, done. The winners build *teams* of agents and let them explore the problem. One agent researches. One drafts. One critiques. One verifies. They hand work between each other, catch each other's mistakes, and surface things a single prompt never would.

You're not writing better prompts. You're designing a system that thinks in steps, checks itself, and runs without you babysitting every output. That's the difference between using AI and actually building with it.

It also changes what you can take on. A solopreneur with a well-built agent team isn't competing with another solopreneur anymore — they're competing with a small company.

## 3. The hard part isn't knowing. It's doing.

Here's the uncomfortable one. None of this is secret. The patterns are public. You could have read all three findings above and learned nothing you couldn't have figured out yourself.

So why is almost nobody doing it?

Because unlocking the full potential of AI is genuinely difficult. It's not a prompt you copy. It's a way of working you have to build, break, and rebuild — figuring out which tasks to hand off, how to structure the team, where to keep a human in the loop, and what to do when it confidently gets something wrong.

Knowing the three findings is easy. Living them is the work. That's exactly why the gap between the top companies and everyone else keeps widening instead of closing.

## Where to go from here

Stop collecting tutorials. Pick one real task this week, build a small agent team around it, and let it run. You'll learn more in that one attempt than in fifty hours of video.
